# 377 — research-num-stab (numerical stability foundational + recent)

## Headline
Reality should explicitly anchor on Higham (2002) + Muller et al. (2018) for backward-error vocabulary, IEEE 754-2019 for the underlying model, and adopt the Higham–Mary (2019) probabilistic √n bound in docs for accumulating algorithms — concretely cited at slots 302/303/308/347.

## Survey

### 1. Higham, "Accuracy and Stability of Numerical Algorithms," 2nd ed., SIAM, 2002 (xxx + 680 pp.)
The single most authoritative reference for finite-precision numerical algorithm analysis. Combines algorithmic derivations, perturbation theory, and rigorous rounding-error analysis. Chapter 1 alone defines the entire vocabulary reality should adopt: relative error, sources of error, precision-vs-accuracy, backward and forward errors, conditioning, cancellation, instability without cancellation, "rounding errors are not random," designing stable algorithms. 2nd-edition additions include symmetric-indefinite + skew-symmetric systems, rank-revealing LU, fused multiply-add (FMA) bounds, and Newton's method for nonlinear systems. Reality-specific use: every "Precision documented, not assumed" docstring should phrase its bound in Higham's `γ_n = nu/(1−nu)` notation. SIAM DOI 10.1137/1.9780898718027.

### 2. Wilkinson, "The Algebraic Eigenvalue Problem," Clarendon/Oxford, 1965 (662 pp.)
Foundational text that established backward error analysis as the dominant tool for assessing algorithm stability. Wilkinson developed the technique on PILOT ACE while solving eigenproblems and only retroactively applied it to Gaussian elimination. The book contains the first rigorous BEA of QR, Householder reduction, inverse iteration, and the symmetric tridiagonal eigenvalue problem. Reality should cite Wilkinson 1965 as the historical source any time it computes eigenvalues (constants/physics modes, control state-space, signal pole-zero) and any time it claims backward stability. A modern restatement is in Higham 2002 ch. 7–8.

### 3. Goldberg, "What Every Computer Scientist Should Know About Floating-Point Arithmetic," ACM Computing Surveys 23(1):5–48, 1991
The canonical introductory paper. Three parts: (a) FP representation and rounding error, (b) IEEE 754 (radix, denormals, exceptions, NaN/Inf semantics, guard digits), (c) systems implications (compilers, languages, exception handling). Defines `ulp` and `relative error` rigorously, proves the round-half-to-even bound, and gives the canonical quadratic-formula and cancellation examples. Required reading for any reality contributor; also the natural reference in `prob/` and `signal/` docstrings whenever an algorithm depends on guard-digit semantics. ACM DOI 10.1145/103162.103163.

### 4. Kahan, "Lecture Notes on the Status of IEEE Standard 754 for Binary Floating-Point Arithmetic," 1997 (work-in-progress) and "How Futile Are Mindless Assessments of Roundoff in Floating-Point Computation?" 2006
Kahan is the principal architect of IEEE 754. The 1997 lecture notes are the practitioner's bible for what 754 actually guarantees (correctly-rounded ±, ×, ÷, √, FMA when present; signed zero; gradual underflow; NaN propagation) and what compilers can break. The 2006 "Mindless" paper warns against statistical / Monte-Carlo approaches to roundoff and motivates analytical BEA — directly relevant to reality slot 303 (relative-error bounds claimed in docstrings must be analytic, not empirical). Kahan also originated compensated summation (slot 302). Available at https://people.eecs.berkeley.edu/~wkahan/.

### 5. IEEE Std 754-2019 (revision of 754-2008)
Approved 13 June 2019. Minor revision but with targeted additions reality should track: augmentedAddition / augmentedSubtraction / augmentedMultiplication (correctly-rounded `(s, t)` such that `a∘b = s+t` exactly — directly enables stable summation and double-double primitives without Dekker/Veltkamp tricks); new `min/max` semantics that are associative and signaling-NaN-clean (fixes a 2008 oversight); `tanPi`, `aSinPi`, `aCosPi` correctly-rounded operations; relaxed `totalOrder` for NaN payloads; `is754version2019` predicate. Reality should target 754-2019 conformance language in docs and use augmented ops where the Go runtime exposes them (currently it does not — `math/bits.Mul64` and software fallbacks suffice for now). https://standards.ieee.org/standard/754-2019.html.

### 6. Sterbenz lemma (Sterbenz, "Floating-Point Computation," 1974)
If `x, y` are non-negative finite floats with `y/2 ≤ x ≤ 2y`, then `x − y` is computed exactly (zero rounding error) under round-to-nearest in any radix-β IEEE format. The lemma is the workhorse behind Dekker's TwoSum / FastTwoSum, the inner step of Kahan summation, double-double subtraction, and Newton-iteration residuals. Used (often implicitly) anywhere reality computes a difference of nearby values without losing accuracy. Should be cited explicitly in `prob/relerr.go`, `signal/correlation.go`, and any `geometry/` SDF using `(p − c)` differences within a half-octave magnitude band. See Wikipedia article and Flocq formal proof at flocq.gitlabpages.inria.fr.

### 7. Carson & Higham, "Accelerating the Solution of Linear Systems by Iterative Refinement in Three Precisions," SIAM J. Sci. Comput. 40(2):A817–A847, 2018 (DOI 10.1137/17M1140819)
Generalizes Wilkinson's iterative refinement to three precisions: `u_f` (factorization), `u` (working), `u_r` (residual). Rounding-error analysis derives convergence conditions and bounds for forward + componentwise/normwise backward error. Headline result: fp16 LU + fp32 working + fp64 residual gives full single-precision accuracy for `κ_∞(A) ≤ 10^4`, with the O(n³) work in half precision. Underpins LAPACK's mixed-precision drivers and the modern AI/HPC convergence. Reality `linalg` doesn't do iterative refinement today, but slot 308 (Kalman square-root) and any future `linalg/itrefine.go` should follow this template; cite explicitly when documenting precision/condition trade-offs.

### 8. Higham & Mary, "A New Approach to Probabilistic Rounding Error Analysis," SIAM J. Sci. Comput. 41(5):A2815–A2835, 2019 (DOI 10.1137/18M1226312)
First rigorous foundation for the long-folkloric "√n rule of thumb": under mild probabilistic assumptions on rounding errors (mean zero, mean independence) and a concentration inequality, the worst-case constant `γ_n = nu/(1−nu)` may be replaced by a constant proportional to `√n·u` with high probability. Applies to inner products, matrix–vector and matrix–matrix products, LU, and Householder QR. Reality should expose this dual bound in docstrings: state both deterministic O(nu) and probabilistic O(√n·u) bounds for every accumulating routine (slot 302 summation, slot 303 relerr, `linalg/matmul.go`, `signal/dft.go`).

### 9. Connolly, Higham & Mary, "Stochastic Rounding and Its Probabilistic Backward Error Analysis," SIAM J. Sci. Comput. 43(1):A566–A585, 2021 (DOI 10.1137/20M1334796)
Proves that *stochastic rounding* (round up/down with probability proportional to distance) produces mean-independent zero-mean rounding errors *unconditionally* — no probabilistic input model required — and so the √n·u backward-error bound holds without assumption. Also eliminates the "stagnation" failure mode of Kahan summation under fixed-precision (small addends obliterated by round-to-nearest when |sum| ≫ |term|). Highly relevant if reality ever simulates fp16/bf16 (slot 347 alternative or AI/Pistachio loop) — stochastic rounding is the rigorous path to recovered accuracy.

### 10. Hida, Li & Bailey, "Library for Double-Double and Quad-Double Arithmetic," LBNL tech report; "Algorithms for Quad-Double Precision," 15th IEEE Symp. Computer Arithmetic, pp. 155–162, 2001 (also revised 2008 manual)
Defines DD (≥106-bit significand, unevaluated sum of two fp64) and QD (≥212-bit) using error-free transformations: TwoSum, FastTwoSum, TwoProd, Split (Dekker–Veltkamp), and Knuth's algorithm. All reduce to Sterbenz + IEEE-754 correctly-rounded `+, ×`. Reality slot 347 should adopt this library's algorithm choices verbatim (and golden-test against QD's reference outputs). The 2008 Bailey/Hida/Li manual at davidhbailey.com/dhbpapers/qd.pdf is the canonical implementation reference.

### 11. Muller et al., "Handbook of Floating-Point Arithmetic," 2nd ed., Birkhäuser, 2018 (632 pp., ISBN 978-3-319-76526-6)
The encyclopedic complement to Higham 2002. Four parts: foundations & history; analyzing/optimizing FP algorithms (correctly-rounded elementary functions, table-maker's dilemma, FMA-based algorithms); IEEE 754-2008 hardware/software implementations; extensions (interval arithmetic, double/triple-word, complex FP, formal verification with Coq/Flocq). The 2nd edition adds GPU-relevant material and FMA-extended-precision. This is the right reference for `geometry/` (correctly-rounded sin/cos/atan2), `calculus/` (FMA-based Horner), and any future formal-verification work. https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-319-76526-6.

### 12. Demmel, "Applied Numerical Linear Algebra," SIAM, 1997 (ISBN 0-89871-389-7)
The pedagogical bridge between Wilkinson 1965 and Higham 2002. Chapters 1–4 give clean proofs of: condition numbers (`κ(A) = ‖A‖·‖A⁻¹‖`), normwise/componentwise backward error, perturbation bounds for Ax=b, eigenvalue conditioning (Bauer–Fike, Davis–Kahan sin Θ), SVD perturbation. Demmel co-designed LAPACK and the book reflects that. Reality should use Demmel's notation in `linalg/cond.go`, `linalg/svd.go` docstrings; Bauer–Fike is the right perturbation bound to quote when a function returns eigenvalues. http://www.stat.uchicago.edu/~lekheng/courses/302/demmel/.

### 13. Higham & Pranesh, "Simulating Low Precision Floating-Point Arithmetic," SIAM J. Sci. Comput. 41(4):C585–C602, 2019 (DOI 10.1137/19M1251308)
Companion software (`chop` library, github.com/higham/chop) for simulating fp16, bfloat16, and arbitrary-precision FP from fp64, with options for subnormals on/off, round-to-nearest / directed / stochastic rounding, and random bit-flips for fault studies. The paper's rounding-error analysis explains why naive simulation can be *more* accurate than native low-precision and how to compensate. Useful precedent if reality ever exposes `prob/lowprec.go` for studying AI/ML quantization sensitivity (Pistachio integration concern).

## Reality slot recommendations

- **Slot 302 (dive-stable-sums)**: Standardize on Neumaier's improved Kahan–Babuška as the default `prob.Sum`, with a `prob.SumPairwise` alternative (O(log n) error vs O(n) naïve). Cite Higham 2002 §4.1–4.4, Kahan original, and Neumaier (1974). Document both deterministic `O(nu)` and probabilistic `O(√n·u)` (Higham–Mary 2019) bounds. Provide TwoSum/FastTwoSum primitives (Sterbenz + Knuth) as exported helpers — they're prerequisites for slot 347.
- **Slot 303 (dive-relerr-bounds)**: Every reality function docstring should quote `γ_n` worst-case, `√n·u` probabilistic (where applicable), and forward + backward error separately. Use Higham 2002 ch. 1 vocabulary verbatim. Audit `prob/`, `signal/`, `linalg/` for empirical-only or absent bounds; replace with analytic bounds derived per Higham 2002 / Demmel 1997.
- **Slot 308 (dive-kalman-square-root)**: Square-root Kalman = Wilkinson-style backward stability for the covariance update. Cite Bierman (1977) UD factorization and Higham 2002 ch. 10 (Cholesky). Augment the existing `linalg.Cholesky` with a downdate primitive; gold-test against double-double reference. Document `κ(P)` rather than `κ(P)²` as the relevant condition number — this is the *raison d'être* of the square-root form.
- **Slot 347 (dive-double-double)**: Implement `dd.Float64` (a, e) following Hida/Li/Bailey 2001/2008 algorithms — TwoSum, FastTwoSum, Dekker–Veltkamp split, TwoProd, then `Add/Sub/Mul/Div/Sqrt`. Cross-validate goldens against QD reference outputs. Use selectively in `orbital/kepler.go` (long-period orbit propagation), `signal/fft.go` (large-N stability), and `prob/lowprec.go` if added. Never use as default — the 5–10× cost is paid only on demand.
- **Cross-cutting**: Add a top-level `docs/precision.md` (or `CONTEXT-PRECISION.md`) that establishes reality's precision contract: target IEEE 754-2019, default round-to-nearest-even, document each function's `γ_n` and `√n·u` bounds. Forward-reference Higham 2002, Goldberg 1991, Kahan 1997, Muller 2018 as required reading.
